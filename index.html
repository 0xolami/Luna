What Your Heart Rate Means
💚
Normal (60–100 BPM)
A resting heart rate of 60–100 BPM is considered healthy for adults. Athletes may naturally sit lower at 40–60 BPM.
⚠️
Elevated (100–120 BPM)
Can indicate stress, dehydration, caffeine, or early illness. Rest, hydrate, and recheck after 10 minutes of calm.
🚨
High (>120 BPM at rest)
Persistent elevated heart rate may warrant medical attention. Track over 2–3 days and consult a doctor if it continues.
🌡️
Cycle & Heart Rate
Your heart rate naturally rises 2–7 BPM after ovulation during the luteal phase. This is normal hormonal variation.

Phase-by-Phase Care
🩸
Menstrual Phase (Days 1–5)
Rest and restore. Reduce intense workouts — opt for yoga or walking. Eat iron-rich foods (spinach, lentils, red meat). Apply a warm compress for cramps. Stay hydrated and prioritise sleep.
🌱
Follicular Phase (Days 6–13)
Energy rises — great time for cardio, strength training, and new projects. Oestrogen peaks here, boosting mood and focus. Eat light, protein-rich meals and fermented foods to support gut health.
✨
Ovulation (Day 14 approx.)
Peak energy and libido. Your social and communication skills are naturally heightened. Great for presentations, dates, and high-intensity training. Stay hydrated — discharge increases slightly.
🌙
Luteal Phase (Days 15–28)
Progesterone rises — you may feel more introspective. Reduce caffeine and sugar to ease PMS. Add magnesium-rich foods (dark chocolate, nuts). Gentle exercise and journaling help mood swings.
Nutrition by Phase
🥩
Iron & Anti-Inflammatory
During your period: leafy greens, red meat, legumes, vitamin C (helps iron absorption), ginger tea for cramps, turmeric.
🥑
Hormone-Supporting Foods
Throughout the cycle: avocados, flaxseeds, salmon, walnuts, broccoli, and cruciferous vegetables support oestrogen balance.
🍫
Luteal Phase Cravings
Dark chocolate (70%+) satisfies cravings while providing magnesium. Reduce salt to prevent bloating. Chamomile tea eases PMS anxiety.
Mental Health
🧘
Managing PMS Mood
Track your emotional patterns to anticipate low days. Regular sleep, limiting alcohol, journaling, and gentle movement significantly reduce PMS symptoms in most people.
🩺
When to See a Doctor
Seek medical advice if: periods are absent for 3+ months, pain is debilitating, bleeding is extremely heavy (>7 days), or you experience severe mood disruption (possible PMDD).
Medications
Your reminders and phase-appropriate options
Recommended for Period Pain
Ibuprofen (NSAIDs)
Best for cramps and inflammation. Take 200–400mg every 4–6 hrs with food. Start at first sign of period. Reduces prostaglandins that cause cramping.
⚠ Avoid if you have stomach ulcers, kidney issues, or asthma. Max 1200mg/day OTC.
Paracetamol (Acetaminophen)
For mild to moderate pain. 500–1000mg every 4–6 hrs. Safer on the stomach than NSAIDs. Can combine with ibuprofen (stagger doses).
⚠ Do not exceed 4000mg/day. Avoid with alcohol or if you have liver concerns.
Buscopan (Hyoscine)
Antispasmodic — relieves uterine cramps specifically. 10mg 3–4 times daily. Works best for spasmodic cramp pain rather than general period pain.
⚠ May cause dry mouth or blurred vision. Not suitable in pregnancy.
Naproxen Sodium
Longer-acting NSAID — lasts 8–12 hrs. 220mg every 8–12 hrs with food. Good for overnight relief without waking to redose.
⚠ Same cautions as ibuprofen. Do not combine with other NSAIDs.
Mefenamic Acid
Prescription NSAID specifically for period pain and heavy bleeding. 500mg 3x daily. Often prescribed for dysmenorrhoea.
⚠ Prescription only in many countries. Take with food. Not for use longer than 3 days.
Magnesium Supplement
Natural muscle relaxant. 200–400mg daily in the luteal phase reduces cramp severity and PMS symptoms over time. Takes weeks to build up.
⚠ High doses may cause loose stools. Glycinate or malate forms are easiest on the stomach.
